In English, there are 8 main parts of speech: nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs, prepositions, conjunctions, and interjections. In this part 1, we will focus on the 4 most basic parts of speech: **nouns**, **verbs**, **adjectives**, and **adverbs**. Understanding these parts of speech clearly will help you build sentences more accurately and richly.

## 1. Nouns

A noun is a word used to refer to a person, place, thing, idea, or concept. There are two main types of nouns: **common nouns** and **proper nouns**.

- **Common nouns**: dog, city, happiness
- **Proper nouns**: John, Paris, Facebook

Nouns can act as subjects, objects, or complements in a sentence. For example:

- **Subject**: The _cat_ is sleeping.
- **Object**: I see a _dog_.
- **Complement**: She is a _teacher_.

Common suffixes to form nouns include:

```ts
-tion, -sion, -ment, -ness, -er, -or, -ship, -ity, -ist, -ism;
```

Meaning of common noun-forming suffixes:

> **`-tion / -sion`** often indicate an action or process.
>
> Example: "information" is the process of conveying information, and "decision" is the process of deciding.

> **`-er / -or`** often indicate the person who performs an action.
>
> Example: "teacher" is a person who teaches, "actor" is a person who acts. However, there are exceptions such as "computer," which is not a person.

> **`-ship`** often indicates a state, condition, or relationship.
>
> Example: "friendship" means the state of being friends, and "leadership" means the quality or role of leading.

> **`-ist`** often indicates a person associated with a belief system or profession.
>
> Example: "artist" is someone who creates art, and "scientist" is someone who does scientific work.

> **`-ism`** often indicates a system of beliefs, a theory, or a movement.
>
> Example: "capitalism" is an economic system, and "realism" is a school of thought or style.

Position of nouns:

- At the beginning of a sentence as the subject: **_Dogs_** are loyal animals.
- Usually after an article (a, an, the) or adjective. Example: a **_book_**, the **_red_** car.
- After a preposition. Example: in the **_house_**, on the **_table_**.
- After a verb. Example: I have a **_dog_**.

> Note: Some nouns can be countable or uncountable. For example, "apple" is countable, while "water" is uncountable.

### Countable and Uncountable Nouns

| Countable nouns                                               | Uncountable nouns                                                                                                                                             |
| ------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Can be counted as separate items: person, tree, car, etc.     | Cannot usually be counted as separate items: liquids, materials, abstract ideas, or mass concepts such as water, air, information, equipment, furniture, etc. |
| Can have singular and plural forms: one book, two books, etc. | Usually used in singular form: we do not normally say "one water" or "two informations."                                                                      |
| Can be used with a/an/the/one: a cat, an apple, etc.          | Do not use a/an directly: we do not say "a water" or "an information."                                                                                        |
| Can be used with numbers: three dogs, five cars, etc.         | Do not usually use direct numbers: we do not say "three waters" or "five informations."                                                                       |
| Often used with: many, a number of, the number of, few/a few  | Often used with: much, a lot of, a great deal of, little/a little                                                                                             |

> Some uncountable nouns look plural in form, such as: news, economics, physics, mathematics.

> Note: Some nouns can be both countable and uncountable depending on context. For example, "chicken" is countable when it means the animal (a chicken, two chickens), but uncountable when it means meat (some chicken, a lot of chicken).

> Some uncountable nouns can be used in countable contexts when referring to types or units. For example, "coffee" is uncountable as a substance, but countable when referring to a type of coffee (a coffee, two coffees) or a serving (a cup of coffee).

### Possessive Case

The **possessive case** is used to express **ownership** or a **relationship** between nouns.

### Key Usage Rules

- **Singular noun**: add **`'s`**

  - _the boy's book_
  - _Mary's car_

- **Plural noun ending in `s`**: add only **`'`**

  - _the students' classroom_
  - _my parents' house_

- **Irregular plural noun** (not ending in `s`): add **`'s`**

  - _children's toys_
  - _men's clothing_

- Use **`of`** for objects, abstract ideas, or long noun phrases

  - _the roof of the house_
  - _the name of the book_

**`'s`** is commonly used for people and animals, while **`of`** is commonly used for objects and abstract ideas. In real usage, however, there are many flexible cases and exceptions depending on context.

## 2. Verbs

A verb is a word that expresses an action, a state, or existence. There are three main types of verbs: **action verbs**, **linking verbs**, and **auxiliary verbs**.

- **Action verbs**: run, eat, write
- **Linking verbs**: be, seem, become
- **Auxiliary verbs**: have, do, will

Verbs can change by tense, person, and number. For example:

- Simple present: I **_eat_** breakfast every day.
- Simple past: She **_ran_** to the store yesterday.
- Simple future: They **_will go_** to the park tomorrow.

Common suffixes to form verbs include:

```ts
-ize, -ise,...
```

Meaning of common verb-forming suffixes:

> **`-ize / -ise`** often indicate making something become a certain way.
>
> Example: "realize" means to become aware of something, and "organize" means to arrange or structure something.

Position of verbs:

- Usually after the subject. Example: The **_cat_** sleeps.
- At the beginning of a question. Example: **_Do_** you like pizza?
- After an auxiliary verb. Example: She **_is running_**.
- After a conjunction. Example: I want to go, but I **_can't_**.

## 3. Adjectives

An adjective is a word used to describe or modify a noun. Adjectives usually appear before nouns or after linking verbs such as "be," "seem," or "become."

Common suffixes to form adjectives include:

```ts
-ous, -ful, -less, -al, -able, -ish, -ic, -ive, -ent;
```

Meaning of common adjective-forming suffixes:

> **`-ous`** often indicates a quality or characteristic.
>
> Example: "dangerous" means full of danger, and "famous" means widely known.

> **`-ful`** often indicates having a lot of a quality.
>
> Example: "beautiful" means full of beauty, and "hopeful" means having hope.

> **`-less`** often indicates lacking something.
>
> Example: "hopeless" means without hope, and "careless" means lacking care.

> **`-al`** often indicates relating to something.
>
> Example: "natural" means related to nature, and "cultural" means related to culture.

> **`-able`** often indicates capability or possibility.
>
> Example: "readable" means able to be read, and "comfortable" means giving comfort.

> **`-ish`** often indicates a somewhat or characteristic quality.
>
> Example: "childish" means like a child, and "foolish" means lacking good sense.

> **`-ic`** often indicates relating to a subject or quality.
>
> Example: "historic" means important in history, and "artistic" means related to art.

> **`-ive / -ent`** often indicate a tendency or quality.
>
> Example: "creative" means having the ability to create, and "independent" means not relying on others.

Position of adjectives:

- Usually before a noun. Example: a **_red_** apple, the **_tall_** building.
- After a linking verb. Example: The sky is **_blue_**. She seems **_happy_**.
- After a noun as part of fixed expressions. Example: "the president **_elect_**," "the heir **_apparent_**."
- After certain prepositions in adjective phrases. Example: "afraid of the **_dark_**," "interested in **_history_**."

Note:

- In a **sentence**, adjectives often come after a form of "to be". In a **noun phrase**, adjectives usually come before the noun. Example: "The movie is **_exciting_**" (in a sentence), "an **_exciting_** movie" (in a noun phrase).

## 4. Adverbs

An adverb is a word used to describe or modify a verb, an adjective, or another adverb.

Adverbs are often formed by adding the suffix "-ly" to adjectives, but there are also many irregular adverbs.

Examples:

- **Adverbs with -ly**: quickly, slowly, happily
- **Irregular adverbs**: well, fast, hard

Position of adverbs:

- Before or after the main verb. Example: She **_quickly_** ran to the store. He ran **_quickly_**.
- Before an adjective or another adverb. Example: She is **_very_** happy. He runs **_extremely_** fast.
- At the beginning or end of a sentence for emphasis. Example: **_Unfortunately_**, we missed the bus. We missed the bus **_unfortunately_**.
